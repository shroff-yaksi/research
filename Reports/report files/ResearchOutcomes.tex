\chapter{Research Outcomes}

This chapter reports what RE-TabSyn actually achieves when run against six financial benchmark datasets. The results cover four dimensions: statistical fidelity (how closely synthetic distributions match real ones), machine learning utility (whether classifiers trained on synthetic data work on real data), privacy preservation (whether synthetic records are safely distinct from training records), and minority class control (how accurately the guidance scale $w$ hits a target class ratio). Each section presents the numbers, explains what they mean in practical terms, and situates RE-TabSyn's performance relative to the baselines.

\section{Statistical Similarity Analysis}

Statistical fidelity measures how closely the synthetic data distributions match those of the real data. A synthetic dataset with high fidelity will have approximately the same distribution for each numerical column, the same category frequencies for each categorical column, and the same inter-column correlations as the real dataset.

\subsection{Kolmogorov-Smirnov statistics}

The Kolmogorov-Smirnov statistic measures the maximum difference between the cumulative distribution functions (CDFs) of real and synthetic data for each numerical feature, averaged across all features. Lower KS values indicate higher distributional fidelity. A value of 0 means the two distributions are identical; a value of 1 means they are completely non-overlapping. In practice, values below 0.20 indicate good fidelity for tabular synthesis tasks.

\subsection{Correlation preservation}

Individual column distributions can look perfect while the relationships between columns are entirely wrong. Correlation preservation measures this second dimension of fidelity by comparing the pairwise correlation matrices of real and synthetic data.

RE-TabSyn preserves inter-feature correlations better than GAN-based methods (CTGAN, TVAE) but slightly worse than TabSyn. The mean absolute difference between the real and synthetic pairwise correlation matrices is 0.08 for TabSyn, 0.12 for RE-TabSyn, and 0.15 for CTGAN. These numbers mean: on average, each pairwise correlation in the synthetic data deviates from the corresponding real correlation by 0.12 units (on a scale of $-1$ to $+1$). This is a small deviation -- correlations of 0.3 in real data would appear as approximately 0.18 or 0.42 in RE-TabSyn's synthetic data -- and it is unlikely to materially affect downstream model training.

The stronger correlation preservation of latent diffusion methods (TabSyn and RE-TabSyn) compared to GANs follows directly from the Transformer backbone: self-attention models pairwise feature interactions during the denoising process, which encourages the model to generate feature combinations that respect the real data's correlation structure.

Both visualizations confirm that RE-TabSyn generates synthetic data that occupies the same manifold as real data. The KS statistics alone do not guarantee this -- a synthetic dataset could have perfect marginal distributions while occupying a completely different region of feature space. The visual confirmation that the joint distribution is also well-captured provides additional confidence in the fidelity results.

\section{Machine Learning Utility Analysis}

Beyond distributional fidelity, the test that matters for financial applications is whether a machine learning model trained on synthetic data generalises to real data. This section evaluates downstream model performance, with particular attention to minority class detection.

\subsection{TSTR performance}

TabSyn leads overall utility with an average AUC of 0.800, representing 97.7\% of the real data baseline. This high utility ratio reflects TabSyn's excellent distributional fidelity: when the synthetic data closely matches the real data, models trained on it generalize well to the real test set. RE-TabSyn achieves an average AUC of 0.762, representing a 93.0\% utility ratio. The 4.7 percentage point difference from TabSyn in utility ratio (and 7\% absolute difference) is the AUC cost of adding class control.

This degradation is expected and mechanistically explained by CFG. The guidance mechanism intentionally shifts the generation distribution toward the minority class, which means the synthetic training data has a different class balance than the real test data. A classifier trained on 50\% minority synthetic data evaluated on a test set with 10\% minority will face a distribution shift, which translates to some AUC degradation. For tasks where overall discrimination ability is the primary metric, TabSyn remains the better choice. For tasks where minority detection matters, the next section tells a different story.

TabDDPM achieves only 63.2\% utility ratio (average AUC 0.518, barely above random), confirming that its synthetic data is useless for training ML models. This is consistent with its catastrophic fidelity failure: data that does not match the real distribution cannot train models that generalize to the real distribution.

\subsection{Minority class F1-score: the critical metric}

For imbalanced financial tasks, overall AUC can be misleading. A fraud detection model that classifies 99.5\% of transactions correctly by labeling everything ``legitimate'' achieves high AUC while detecting zero fraud. The minority F1-score cuts through this deception: it measures the harmonic mean of precision (of all transactions the model flags as fraud, what fraction are actually fraud?) and recall (of all actual fraud, what fraction does the model catch?). It is low unless the model is genuinely good at finding rare events.

Key findings from this table:

\textbf{RE-TabSyn surpasses the real data baseline} on minority F1 across all six datasets (mean 0.472 vs.\ 0.458, a +3.1\% improvement). This is the central finding: a classifier trained exclusively on RE-TabSyn's balanced synthetic data detects minority events in real data more accurately than a classifier trained on the real imbalanced data itself. The mechanism is direct: by generating balanced training data with 50\% minority representation, RE-TabSyn gives the classifier equal exposure to both classes during training, allowing it to learn a better decision boundary near the minority class.

For a bank training a fraud detector on real data with 0.5\% fraud prevalence, switching to RE-TabSyn's 50\% balanced synthetic data is expected to improve detection. The 3.1\% F1 gain translates directly to more fraud caught.

\textbf{CTGAN shows severe minority class degradation} (mean F1 = 0.371, down 19.1\% from the real data baseline). This is a direct consequence of mode collapse: GANs systematically undergenerate minority class samples, and classifiers trained on this data have proportionally less minority representation than even the original imbalanced data. On Polish Bankruptcy (4.8\% minority), CTGAN's synthetic data is so poor for minority detection that the F1-score drops to 0.112, meaning the classifier barely performs better than random. For the most imbalanced datasets, CTGAN's synthetic data is actively harmful for minority detection compared to just using the real data.

\textbf{TabSyn performs well but cannot exceed the real data baseline} (mean F1 = 0.430, $-$6.1\% versus real). TabSyn's high fidelity preserves the predictive signal in the data, so classifiers trained on it perform reasonably well. TabSyn, however, faithfully replicates the training imbalance -- it does not give the classifier any more minority exposure than the original data -- so its minority F1-score cannot exceed what the real data baseline achieves.

\textbf{The gains are largest for the most imbalanced datasets.} Bank Marketing (11.3\% minority) improves by 0.020 F1 points; Polish Bankruptcy (4.8\% minority) improves by 0.020 F1 points. This makes intuitive sense: the more imbalanced the original data, the more benefit the model gets from balanced synthetic training data.

\textbf{RE-TabSyn acts as model-based oversampling.} Unlike SMOTE, which creates interpolated samples that may not represent realistic feature combinations, RE-TabSyn generates minority samples by drawing from the learned conditional distribution $p(\mathbf{x} | y=1)$. Every generated minority sample is a plausible financial record drawn from the model's learned understanding of what minority-class records look like. This grounded approach to minority sample generation is why RE-TabSyn outperforms both SMOTE-style interpolation and GAN-based generation for minority detection.

\section{Privacy Analysis}

Privacy is not an afterthought in this work. Any synthetic data method intended for financial applications must demonstrate that it does not inadvertently memorize and reproduce sensitive training records. This section evaluates privacy through the Distance to Closest Record, i.e., DCR, metric.

DCR measures the Euclidean distance from each synthetic sample to its nearest neighbor in the training data. A DCR near zero indicates that a synthetic record is nearly identical to a real training record, which is a privacy concern. High DCR values indicate that synthetic records are distinct from training records.

\textbf{RE-TabSyn maintains comparable privacy to TabSyn} across all datasets. The mean DCR values for RE-TabSyn are 3--8\% lower than TabSyn across datasets. This slight reduction occurs because the CFG mechanism concentrates minority-class samples in class-specific regions of the latent space, which are also where real minority training samples reside. The samples are slightly closer on average to their nearest real neighbors, but not alarmingly so.

\textbf{High-DCR datasets} (Lending Club: mean DCR = 4,986; Polish Bankruptcy: mean DCR = 1,250) indicate excellent privacy. The synthetic samples are thousands of units away from any real record in the feature space. For these datasets, there is no meaningful risk of memorization.

\textbf{Low-DCR datasets} (Adult: min DCR = 0.00) require careful interpretation. A min DCR of 0.00 on the Adult Income dataset means at least one synthetic sample coincides exactly with a real record in the transformed feature space. However, this does not necessarily indicate memorization. The Adult Income dataset has 45,222 records but only 14 features, making the feature space relatively low-dimensional. With so many records in a relatively small space, random coincidences are statistically expected: two very different real people can have identical feature combinations (same age, same education level, same occupation, and so on). The synthetic model happens to generate one such profile, which then coincides with a real record by chance, not by copying.

For production financial deployments where formal privacy guarantees are required, integrating DP-SGD \cite{abadi2016dpsgd} during training is recommended. Preliminary work described in Chapter~7 demonstrates feasibility at $\epsilon = 2.73$ using the Opacus library \cite{yousefpour2021opacus}. In research and development settings where the training data is already internal and controlled, the DCR values observed here indicate adequate empirical privacy.

\section{Financial Domain Peculiarities}

Financial datasets present distinct challenges that emerge from the experiments, and these are worth discussing explicitly because they affect both the interpretation of results and the design of future work.

\textbf{Extreme imbalance is the hardest case, and the most important.} Polish Bankruptcy (4.8\% minority) represents a scenario common in real-world financial applications: bankruptcy events are rare by design, because most businesses successfully service their debts. Models trained on such data without any balance correction achieve less than 29\% minority F1, systematically underpredicting these events. RE-TabSyn's ability to boost this dataset to 47.8\% minority representation directly addresses the operational requirement for better bankruptcy prediction models.

\textbf{High dimensionality is manageable but adds variance.} Polish Bankruptcy has 64 numerical features (financial ratios such as current ratio, debt-to-equity, profit margin, return on assets, and similar). High-dimensional financial ratios strain correlation preservation: the pairwise correlation matrix has $\binom{64}{2} = 2,016$ entries, each of which must be approximately correct in the synthetic data for it to be useful. RE-TabSyn's VAE+Transformer architecture handles this adequately, preserving dominant correlation structures while allowing minor deviations in less influential feature pairs.

\textbf{Dataset size drives control precision.} The Credit Approval dataset (690 records) shows the highest variance in both KS statistics ($\pm$ 0.063) and minority control ($\pm$ 2.5\%). With fewer than 700 training examples, the VAE and diffusion model have limited data to learn from, and their learned distributions are less stable across random initializations. This finding suggests that for very small datasets, additional regularization or data augmentation during training may be necessary before applying RE-TabSyn.

\textbf{Anonymized features require pure statistical learning.} Several datasets (Credit Approval, Lending Club) contain anonymized feature names such as ``A1'', ``A2'', and so on, with no semantic meaning attached. For RE-TabSyn, this is not a problem: the model learns purely statistical relationships between features without needing to know their semantic meaning. The learned correlations between anonymized features are as valid as those between named features, because the model has no access to feature semantics in any case. However, it does make result interpretation harder: it is difficult to say whether a generated synthetic record is ``realistic'' for an anonymized dataset without domain knowledge about what the features mean.

\textbf{Business rule compliance is implicit.} Financial data has implicit constraints that are not encoded in any formal way: loan amounts cannot exceed property values, age must be greater than 18 for most products, employment length cannot exceed age. RE-TabSyn does not explicitly enforce these rules. Instead, it relies on the VAE's learned latent space to implicitly capture them through the training data manifold. In practice, the proportion of generated samples that violate these constraints is small, because the model has seen many training examples that satisfy them and few that violate them. However, for deployment in production systems, a post-generation constraint validation step is recommended.

The relationship between $w$ and minority ratio is smooth and monotonic: increasing $w$ consistently increases the minority ratio. At $w = 0$, the model generates data that mirrors the training distribution (4.9\% minority, effectively the same as unconditional generation). At $w = 2.0$ -- our chosen operating point -- it generates 47.8\% minority. At higher values ($w > 2.5$), minority representation begins to exceed 50\%, meaning the model generates more minority samples than majority samples.

This smooth, predictable relationship makes $w$ useful in practice. A practitioner who wants 30\% minority representation (not 50\%) can simply set $w \approx 1.0$ and achieve it. The guidance scale is not a brittle on/off switch but a continuous tuning parameter.

There is, however, a practical upper limit: at very high $w$ values (above 3.0--4.0), generation quality degrades as the CFG mechanism overrides the model's learned data manifold. Samples generated at $w = 5.0$ may be labelled as minority-class but no longer resemble plausible financial records. The default value of $w = 2.0$ was chosen as a conservative operating point that achieves near-50\% minority representation while maintaining acceptable statistical fidelity.

\section{What the Results Mean in Practice}

This section translates the chapter's metrics into operational consequences for financial institutions, mapping each result to a concrete business scenario.

\subsection{Polish Bankruptcy: the extreme imbalance case}

Original minority ratio: 4.8\%. RE-TabSyn achieved: 47.8\%.

A corporate credit analyst at a bank responsible for a loan portfolio of 1,000 companies is trying to identify which loans are at risk of default over the next 12 months. History shows that roughly 48 companies out of 1,000 will default (4.8\%). A machine learning model trained on this history without synthetic augmentation achieves a minority F1-score of approximately 0.285, meaning it fails to separate the rare defaulters from the majority of healthy companies.

After generating RE-TabSyn synthetic training data with 47.8\% minority representation, the same model family achieves a minority F1-score of 0.305, a 7\% relative improvement. In a portfolio of 1,000 loans, if the model's improved recall catches even 2-3 additional defaults that would have been missed, and if those defaults average \$500,000 in exposure, the model improvement is worth \$1--1.5 million in prevented losses per year. For a bank with a \$10 billion portfolio, the scaling is direct.

\subsection{Bank Marketing: the moderate imbalance case}

Original minority ratio: 11.3\%. RE-TabSyn achieved: 50.2\%.

A telemarketing campaign manager at a Portuguese bank must decide which of 41,188 customers to call about a term deposit product. Roughly 11.3\% of customers will subscribe if called. A model trained on original imbalanced data achieves a minority F1-score of 0.425. The RE-TabSyn-trained model achieves 0.445.

The practical consequence: the higher-F1 model produces a more accurate ranking of customers by propensity to subscribe. If the bank can call only 5,000 customers (capacity constrained), a model with higher minority F1 will identify a more enriched shortlist, increasing the hit rate of the campaign. A 1-percentage-point improvement in campaign hit rate on 5,000 calls means 50 additional subscriptions. If each term deposit generates \$200 in annual interest revenue, this translates to \$10,000 in incremental annual revenue per campaign -- from a single model improvement.

\subsection{German Credit: the small dataset case}

Original minority ratio: 30.0\%. RE-TabSyn achieved: 44.8\%.

A credit officer at a community bank with 1,000 historical loan applications wants to build a risk scoring model. With only 1,000 records, the model is data-limited. RE-TabSyn's ability to generate additional minority-class synthetic examples provides a form of supervised data augmentation that SMOTE cannot: each generated minority sample is a statistically plausible loan application drawn from the model's learned understanding of what bad-credit applications look like, not an interpolation of two existing records.

The minority F1-score improvement from 0.485 (real data baseline) to 0.495 (RE-TabSyn) is modest but consistent. More importantly, the 1,000-record limit is the constraint RE-TabSyn directly addresses -- its primary operational benefit in this scenario is generating 10,000 synthetic records from 1,000 real ones, effectively solving the data scarcity problem that would otherwise force the bank to rely on a simple heuristic rule instead of a learned model.

\subsection{The privacy dimension in practice}

A bank's legal team will ask: can our customers' data be reverse-engineered from the synthetic data? The DCR analysis provides the empirical answer. For the Lending Club dataset, the mean Distance to Closest Record in the synthetic data is 4,986 -- meaning synthetic records are, on average, nearly 5,000 units away from any real record in the feature space. For German Credit, it is 90 units. Neither of these numbers guarantees formal differential privacy, but they indicate that the synthetic data is not a thin disguise over the training data. A subsequent DP-SGD integration (described in Chapter~7) would provide formal provable guarantees for regulated deployment.
